Understanding the pathophysiology of neurological symptoms observed after severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV2) infection is essential to optimizing outcomes and therapeutics. To date, small sample sizes and narrow molecular profiling have limited the generalizability of findings. In this study, we profiled multiple cortical and subcortical regions in postmortem brains of patients with coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) and controls with matched pulmonary pathology (total n = 42) using spatial transcriptomics, bulk gene expression and proteomics. We observed a multi-regional antiviral response without direct active SARS-CoV2 infection. We identified dysregulation of mitochondrial and synaptic pathways in deep-layer excitatory neurons and upregulation of neuroinflammation in glia, consistent across both mRNA and protein. Remarkably, these alterations overlapped substantially with changes in age-related neurodegenerative diseases, including Parkinson’s disease and Alzheimer’s disease. Our work, combining multiple experimental and analytical methods, demonstrates the brain-wide impact of severe acute/subacute COVID-19, involving both cortical and subcortical regions, shedding light on potential therapeutic targets within pathways typically associated with pathological aging and neurodegeneration.

The syndrome associated with coronavirus infection caused by severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV2) (coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19)) is a complex medical condition first detected in humans in 2019 (ref. 1). A wide range of debilitating neurological and psychiatric symptoms have been reported in patients with acute and subacute COVID-19, including headache, dizziness, anosmia, encephalitis, encephalopathy and psychiatric syndromes2-7. Chronically, these symptoms could progress to include problems with memory, concentration, alterations in skin sensation, autonomic dysfunction, intractable fatigue, delusions and paranoia in recovered patients and those with long COVID (refs. 2,8,9).

Characterizing brain pathology, both histologically and molecularly using modern genomic methods, as has been done in other neuropsychiatric and neurodegenerative disorders10-14, is critical for understanding disease mechanisms and developing effective therapeutics. Several studies performed histological examinations of postmortem brain patients with COVID-19 and reported a variety of changes, including reactive microglial and astrocytic proliferation, cortical or subcortical infarcts, intracranial bleeding, hypoxic-ischemic injury and occasional necrotizing encephalitis and vasculitis15-20. Radiological studies have also revealed non-specific white matter hyperintensities, hypoperfusion and signs of ischemic events in patients with COVID-19 (refs. 21,22). Bulk tissue RNA sequencing studies of postmortem brains found immune response dysregulation and complement activation in the olfactory bulb and amygdala23 and the presence of aging-like effects of COVID-19 in the frontal cortex24. At the single-cell level, one single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) study of the frontal cortex in patients with COVID-19 revealed alterations in upper-layer excitatory neurons and microglia and overlaps in glial profiles with neurodegenerative diseases25. Another snRNA-seq study identified cortical microglia activation without significant changes in cell type composition26.

Although these studies offer initial clues and valuable insights, in most instances, the findings are limited by the relatively small sample size and incomplete matching between cases and controls for potential confounding factors, such as pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes, which likely contribute to the inconsistencies in results across studies. It remains unclear whether these changes are due to systemic comorbidities or are specific to COVID-19. Moreover, considering the potential of COVID-19 to impact various aspects of brain function, more generalizable findings would be informed by profiling different molecular species in multiple brain regions in parallel. Furthermore, given the brain’s cellular heterogeneity, molecular profiling with retained cellular spatial information is necessary for a more comprehensive understanding of COVID-19 pathophysiology.

To address these gaps, we performed multi-omics analyses in multiple brain regions, including proteomics in eight brain regions and bulk tissue RNA gene expression analysis (nCounter)27 in four brain regions of patients with COVID-19 and controls. We complemented these bulk tissue analyses with spatial transcriptomic profiling28 in three cortical and subcortical brain structures—frontal lobe, pons and midbrain—to understand tissue involvement at the cellular level and the nature of gray matter and white matter changes (Fig.1a). We applied high-dimensional weighted gene co-expression network analysis (hdW-GCNA)29,30 to integrate our spatial transcriptomics data with published snRNA-seq results, allowing us to identify consistent patterns of gene dysregulation across studies. By employing this unbiased sampling and multi-omics approach, while controlling for pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes, we identified brain-wide molecular alterations associated with severe acute/subacute COVID-19, uncovering more generalizable insights into the mechanisms underlying the neurological manifestations of the disease.

Results

Our patient cohort consisted of 42 individuals, 22 non-COVID controls and 20 patients with COVID-19 (Fig.1a), matched by age, gender, postmortem interval, immunocompromised status and ventilation as well as pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes. All patients had acute/subacute COVID-19 with disease duration less than 60 d and were diagnosed with either antemortem or postmortem nasopharyngeal or bronchial swab polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for SARS-CoV2 RNA. Histological examination of the lungs showed that 94.7% (18/20) of the patients with COVID-19 manifested the acute and/or organizing phase of lung injury with superimposed bacterial/fungal pneumonia and pulmonary thromboembolism; 77.3% (17/22) of the control individuals also had pulmonary pathology, including acute pulmonary thromboembolism and aspiration/bacterial/fungal pneumonia, which was not significantly different (P = 4.14 × 10−1, Fisher’s exact test, two-tailed). Histological examination of postmortem brains also showed 50% (10/20) of patients had acute anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes in the hippocampi, and 40.9% (9/22) of the controls had acute anoxic-ischemic pathology in the same region (P = 7.57 × 10−1, Fisher’s exact test, two-tailed), consistent with reasonable overall matching of cases and controls for these key confounders (see Supplementary Table 1 for clinical and histopathological characteristics).

We began by evaluating molecular changes in both cortical and subcortical regions to identify the regions most affected by COVID-19. In bulk tissue proteomics, out of eight regions profiled, frontal lobe and pons showed the most significant alterations (Benjamini–Hochberg (BH)-adjusted P < 0.1, MSstatsTMT31; Extended Data Fig. 1a). At the RNA level, nCounter bulk tissue direct gene expression assay27 (targeting 825 genes; Methods) in four brain regions revealed that frontal lobe had the most significant changes, with 319 genes differentially expressed (DE) (BH-adjusted P < 0.1, DESeq2 (ref. 32); Extended Data Fig. 1a).

We compared these results with data from spatial transcriptomics (GeoMx DSP28; Methods), which conducted an unbiased genome-wide assessment of approximately 18,000 transcripts, and allowed segmenting brain tissue into anatomically distinct areas based on gray and white matter, including frontal gray matter (layer 2/3 (L2/3) to layer 6 (L6)), frontal white matter, pontine nuclei, pontocerebellar tracts, corticospinal tracts and pontine tegmentum (Fig.1b,c). We focused on the tissue sections without major changes in integrity, such as large infarcts, hemorrhages or bacterial/fungal infections, to minimize their influence and ensure that the observed changes were not directly caused by secondary tissue damage. Principal component analysis (PCA) showed that the first principal component (PC1) of DE genes (BH-adjusted P < 0.1, linear mixed model (LMM), lme4 (ref. 33)) had the strongest correlation with COVID-19 diagnosis; other factors, such as age, gender, postmortem interval, disease duration, ventilation, immunocompromised status and batch effect, did not significantly impact the results (Supplementary Fig. 1). Histological examination showed no significant differences in gray matter and white matter of the frontal lobe and pons between the non-COVID controls and the patients with COVID-19 (Supplementary Table 2). However, spatial transcriptomic profiling revealed that most DE genes were in the frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, compared to white matter tracts (Extended Data Fig. 1a). In total, 1,437 DE genes were shared between frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei (P = 2.07 × 10−71, odds ratio (OR) = 2.07, GeneOverlap34) and altered similarly (Fig.1d; Pearson’s correlation, two-sided, r = 0.81, P = 0.00 × 100), suggesting shared impact on gray matter in different regions of the brain. In addition, there were 163 shared DE genes between frontal lobe gene expression (nCounter) and the frontal gray matter (P = 1.60 × 10−11, OR = 2.14, GeneOverlap34), which showed similar alterations (Pearson’s correlation, two-sided, r = 0.77, P = 1.62 × 10−33) (Extended Data Fig. 1b). In contrast, frontal white matter had only three shared DE genes with the frontal bulk profile (P = 0.60, OR = 0.98, GeneOverlap34) and no significant expression correlation (Pearson’s correlation, two-sided, r = −0.42, P = 0.72) (Extended Data Fig. 1b), suggesting that the bulk tissue gene expression was primarily driven by gray matter changes in the frontal lobe.

Low viral load in the postmortem brains of acute COVID-19

We next sought to understand whether these changes were concomitant with and, thus, potentially caused by persistent viral infection of the brain. nCounter tissue direct gene expression analyses of nine SARS-CoV2 probes, targeting genes encoding nucleocapsid (N), spike (S), membrane (M) and envelope (E) proteins as well as ORF1ab, ORF1ab_rev, ORF3a, ORF7a and ORF8 genes, showed no significant upregulation in patients compared to non-diseased controls (Extended Data Fig. 1c). Similarly, no viral proteins were detected in the proteomic analysis. In the more sensitive spatial transcriptomics assay, we used three probes, including probe S, targeting the spike protein mRNA and probes ORF1ab and ORF1ab_rev targeting ORF1ab mRNA and the reverse complement of ORF1ab, respectively, to reflect the transcription status of the SARS-CoV2 virus. We found a small, yet significant, upregulation of these viral probes in the frontal gray matter and pontine gray matter, coupled with upregulation of several antiviral defense genes, indicating the presence of low levels of viral mRNA in brain (Fig.1e).

The molecular mechanisms by which SARS-CoV2 might infect brain tissue are not yet fully understood. Outside of the brain, SARS-CoV2 uses the canonical receptor angiotensin-converting enzyme-2 (ACE2), as well as non-canonical receptors basigin (BSG) and neuropilin-1 (NRP1) for cell entry35,36. The spike protein of the virus is cleaved by proteases including cathepsin B and L (CTSB and CTSL), transmembrane serine protease 11 and 2 (TMPRSS11A/B and 2) and FURIN, which are all involved in viral cell entry and replication37. Multiple genes involved in antiviral defense mechanisms have been identified16,25. We, therefore, sought to characterize the expression levels of these SARS-CoV2-related genes. As depicted in Fig.1e, we observed upregulation of ACE2, FURIN and NRP1 in the frontal gray matter, coupled with the presence of low viral mRNA expression. Anti-viral genes IFITM1, IFITM2 and IFITM3 were upregulated in the frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, and IFITM3 was upregulated in the corticospinal tract and pontocerebellar tract, indicating activation of these antiviral response genes in multiple brain regions. These results are consistent with the recent snRNA-seq study in frontal cortex of patients with COVID-19, which found evidence for the antiviral response in brain, although without detection of viral mRNA25.

Given the mild elevation of SARS-CoV2 docking and processing genes (ACE2, FURIN and NRP1) at the transcriptomic level, we investigated protein-level alterations. Using anti-ACE2 and FURIN immunohistochemistry (IHC) in non-COVID controls (n = 8) and patients with COVID-19 (n = 9), we identified mildly elevated protein expression in vascular/perivascular structures within the frontal white matter (P = 3.95 × 10−2 for ACE2; P = 3.00 × 10−3 for FURIN, unpaired t-test, one-tailed) (Extended Data Fig. 2a,b). In non-COVID controls, anti-NRP1 IHC indicated predominant astrocytic expression in five samples and neuronal expression in three. In patients with COVID-19, NRP1 immunoreactivity was seen in both neurons and astrocytes with increased neuronal immunoreactivity in the nucleus of four patients and in the cytosol of two patients (Extended Data Fig. 3a). The overall immunoreactive area was slightly, yet significantly, elevated in patients with COVID-19 (P = 3.20 × 10−3, unpaired t-test, one-tailed). These findings suggest that there was a mildly increased expression of SARS-CoV2 cell entry-related proteins, consistent with the transcriptomic results.

Neuronal/glial dysregulation in the frontal lobe and pons

To link cell-type-specific changes to the increased antiviral response observed in the frontal lobe and pons, we performed expression weighted cell type enrichment (EWCE)38 analysis on the DE genes from frontal gray and white matter, pontine nuclei, corticospinal tract, pontocerebellar tract and tegmentum (Methods). We annotated the cell types according to recent human cortical snRNA-seq studies: Wamsley et al. (31 controls)39, Yang et al. (seven non-flu/non-COVID-19 controls)25 and Bakken et al. (two non-disease donors)40. We performed enrichment of major cell types for all areas and layer-based subclassification in the frontal lobe (Methods and Fig.1f,g). Downregulated genes were enriched in the both superficial and deep-layer excitatory neurons in frontal cortex with a greater enrichment in L5/6 excitatory neurons (all false discovery rate (FDR)-adjusted P < 9.00 × 10−3 in Fig. 1f,g), including a group of non-layer-specific neurons marked by NRGN expression, illustrated in Yang et al.25 and Velmeshev. et al.41 (Methods and Extended Data Fig. 1d). Similar downregulation was observed in excitatory neurons of pontine nuclei (FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5; Fig.1f) and, thus, not restricted to superficial layer cortical neurons, as previously concluded25. Astrocytic markers were upregulated in the frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, whereas microglia and blood–brain barrier (BBB) cell markers were upregulated across the gray and white matter (all FDR-adjusted P < 0.05; Fig. 1f,g). Oligodendrocyte markers were also significantly upregulated in the corticospinal tract and pontocerebellar tract (FDR-adjusted P < 9.00 × 10−3; Fig.1f). Together, these results indicate a multiregional downregulation of neuronal genes and an upregulation of glial genes across both cortical and subcortical regions in patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19.

WGCNA reveals shared mechanisms with neurodegenerative diseases

To organize the profound, cell-type-specific gene expression changes in frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, we performed a consensus WGCNA to identify coherent biological pathways based on co-expressed genes in the frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei. Expression datasets from 95 regions of interest (ROIs) of both areas were subjected to normalization and linear regression to subtract effects of batch, sequencing, age, postmortem interval and ventilation status, followed by module clustering (Methods and Extended Data Fig. 4a). Twenty-two consensus modules showed significant differences between controls and patients with COVID-19, and 16 modules had specific cell type enrichment patterns (Extended Data Fig. 4b-d).

The largest turquoise module, containing 650 and 578 genes downregulated in frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, respectively (Fig. 2a), was enriched for deep-layer excitatory neuron markers (standard deviation from means (sd_from_mean) = 25.59, FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5, EWCE38; Extended Data Fig. 4b). The top Gene Ontology (GO) terms showed enrichment for genes implicated in multiple neurodegenerative diseases, particularly Parkinson’s disease (PD), oxidative phosphorylation and synaptic vesicle pathways (Fig. 2a; all BH-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−22, Metascape42). Protein–protein interaction (PPI) analysis of genes with top kME values (kME > 0.91) in the turquoise module also revealed a highly significant interaction network (P < 1.10 × 10−16, STRING43) with HSP90AB1, ATP5A1, NDUFS5, TPI1, SYP, APLP2, ATP2A2, CALM3 and YWHAZ as top hub genes (Fig. 2b). Module greenyellow corresponded to astrocytes (sd_from_mean = 6.21; FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5, EWCE38; Extended Data Fig. 4b) and was enriched in genes associated with metal binding metallothioneins (MT1A, MT1E, MT1F, MT1G, MT1M, MT1X and MT2A, BH-adjusted P = 4.31 × 10−10, Metascape42) and negative regulation of fatty acid/organic acid transport (Fig. 2c,d). Metallothionein proteins respond to oxidative stress and have been associated with both neuroprotective and neurodegenerative processes44.

The green module had upregulated BBB/endothelial cell markers (sd_from_mean = 3.01, FDR-adjusted P = 1.69 × 10−2, EWCE38; Extended Data Fig. 4b) and was enriched in NABA matrisome-associated pathway (CST4 and ITIH5 as top hub genes) and oxidative stress-induced senescence (BH-adjusted P = 9.73 × 10−3 and P = 7.59 × 10−4, respectively, Metascape42) (Fig. 2e,f). Moreover, histone modification and DNA methylation were also enriched (Fig. 2e). Oxidative stress45-47, matrisome-associated pathway48,49 and epigenetic regulation50,51 have all been linked to aging and age-related diseases.

The blue module contained upregulated microglia and BBB/endothelial cell markers (FDR-adjusted P = 2.4 × 10−4 and P = 6.60 × 10−3, EWCE38; Extended Data Fig. 4b) and was enriched in pathway of lipid transport (BH-adjusted P = 6.04 × 10−3, Metascape42; Fig. 2g,h). Activation of microglia and endothelial cells and dysregulation of lipid metabolism have been observed in both acute and chronic brain injury and across a multitude of neurodegenerative diseases48,52-55. Interestingly, although with weaker significance, pathways related to bacterial infections, such as Staphylococcus aureus infection, GPCR ligand binding and response to bacteria, were also enriched in the blue module (BH-adjusted P = 4.92 × 10−2, 4.95 × 10−2 and 5.57 × 10−2, Metascape42; Fig. 2g), suggesting potential overlap with mechanisms of brain response to pathogen. Notably, the antiviral defense gene IFITM1 was in this blue module, and IFITM3 was among the top hub genes in the astrocytic module. These results suggest a concomitant association between the antiviral response and upregulation of metallothionein-related, NABA matrisome-associated, lipid metabolism and pathogen response pathways in glial and endothelial cells within both cortical and subcortical gray matter.

Using hdWGCNA29,30, the turquoise and greenyellow modules were projected onto the uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) plots of the frontal cortical snRNA-seq results from eight COVID-19 cases and seven controls (Yang et al.25). Consistent with the EWCE results (Extended Data Fig. 4b-d), the turquoise module genes were expressed in excitatory neurons, including NRGN neurons. Module greenyellow genes were expressed within astrocytes (Fig. 3a), validating our annotations. We further performed WGCNA within excitatory neurons and NRGN neurons (Fig. 3b-g and Supplementary Fig. 2). Nine out of 10 excitatory neuronal modules and 12 out of 18 NRGN neuronal modules were significantly altered in the patients with COVID-19 (Fig. 3b-d and Supplementary Fig. 2a,b). The top downregulated module (ExNeuN-M3), which was preferentially expressed in both excitatory and NRGN neurons (Fig. 3e; hdWGCNA30), had the top GO enrichment terms of multiple neurodegenerative diseases, including Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and PD, as well as oxidative phosphorylation and synaptic vesicle pathways (Fig. 3f; Metascape42). The top hub genes of ExNeuN-M3—PEBP1, CALM3, VAMP2 and HSP90AB1—were also the top hub genes identified in the spatial transcriptomic turquoise module (Figs. 2b and 3g; hdWGCNA30). In NRGN neurons, one downregulated module (NRGN-M2) enriched in similar pathways was also identified (Supplementary Fig. 2b,c; EnrichR56). Together, the remarkable similarity between the biological processes in the gray matter from patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19 and those seen in multiple neurodegenerative diseases suggests overlap in pathogenesis.

Pervasive mitochondrial and synaptic dysfunction at RNA level

We next asked whether these biological processes observed in gray matter were also observed in white matter. Comparison of DE genes from six areas of frontal and pons showed that, although each anatomic distinct area had its unique differential gene expression pattern, a substantial number of overlapping genes were shared by three or more areas (Fig. 4a; Circos57). The frontal gray matter, pontine nuclei and pontocerebellar tract had significant downregulation in pathways observed in PD, synaptic organization, vesicle-mediated transport and chemical synaptic transmission (Fig. 4b), whereas the frontal white matter and corticospinal tract had much less alteration in these pathways (Fig. 4b), consistent with the paucity of neuronal cell bodies in these structures.

We also leveraged PPI networks using orthogonal data, applying the Molecular Complex Detection (MCODE) algorithm58 to identify densely connected clusters from the full network (Methods). Figure 4c displays the merged PPI and MCODE networks (Metascape42) of DE genes in six areas. Cluster 1 genes, including NSF, SNAP25, STXBP1, VAMP2, SYT1, CPLX1, SYN1, SYN2, GOT1, ATP6V1B2, ATP6V1G2, ATP6V0E2, ATP5F1A, ATP5F1B, ATP5MC2 and ATP6AP1, were associated with synaptic vesicle cycle and mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (BH-adjusted P = 1.00 × 10−14 and 2.00 × 10−9). Cluster 2 genes (OGDHL, HMGCS1, PGAM4, ATP6V1A, SLC25A4, PPP3CB, SLC25A6, ANXA6, GDI1 and EIF4A2) were associated with ribonucleotide and ATP metabolic process (BH-adjusted P = 1.58 × 10−4 and 2.51 × 10−4). Cluster 3 genes (AP2M1, NDUFA4, DNAJC6, YWHAH, HSPA1B, NDUFA8, UQCRHL, ATP2B2, HSPA8 and ATP9A) were associated with prion disease and endocytosis (BH-adjusted P = 1.00 × 10−6 and 3.16 × 10−5). Cluster 4 genes (NDUFB2, UQCRB, COX7A2L, COX4I1, SLC25A23 and CST3) were associated with electronic transport chain and PD (BH-adjusted P = 5.01 × 10−8 and 3.16 × 10−6). Cluster 5 genes (SPTBN2, SCN1A and SCN8A) were associated with L1CAM interactions (BH-adjusted P = 2.00 × 10−6). Overall, two distinct analyses, WGCNA and MCODE, both revealed prominent synaptic and mitochondrial defects observed in age-related neurodegenerative conditions.

Fewer upregulated genes were shared across multiple areas (Fig. 4d). Consistent with co-expression analysis (Fig. 2), the NABA matrisome-associated pathway and the immune response and systemic lupus erythematosus-related pathways were specifically upregulated in cortical and subcortical gray matter. Besides NABA matrisome pathways, upregulation of immune response genes has been observed in the frontal and temporal cortex of multiple neurodegenerative diseases, including frontotemporal dementia (FTD), corticobasal degeneration (CBD) and progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP)48,59. Together, these results suggest that cortical and subcortical excitatory neurons manifest substantial downregulation of genes involved in mitochondria respiration and synaptic vesicle function, accompanied by the upregulation of immune response in glial cells and BBB/endothelial cells. Again, these pathways show significant overlap in both content and directionality with pathways observed in neurodegenerative diseases.

Proteomics validate and expand spatial transcriptomic results

To validate our spatial transcriptomics findings in bulk tissue RNA, we conducted a cell type enrichment analysis (EWCE38) on DE genes from the frontal lobe nCounter results (Supplementary Fig. 3 and Methods). Consistently, we found downregulation of excitatory neuron markers, especially in deep-layer excitatory neurons, as well as significant downregulation of interneurons (Fig. 5a). Additionally, there was an upregulation of oligodendrocytes, microglia and BBB/endothelial cells (Fig. 5a).

To assess if transcriptomic changes are reflected at the protein level, we focused on the proteomic results of the frontal lobe and pons, which exhibited the most significant changes (Extended Data Fig. 1a). For each region, we used tandem mass tag (TMT) 16plex labeling and CMMB-based isopropanol gradient peptide fractionation (CIF) to profile seven non-COVID controls and nine patients with COVID-19 in a single reaction, ensuring random and equal labeling of both groups (Methods). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA60,61; Methods) of frontal lobe changes revealed prominent downregulation of mitochondrial function, including the citric acid tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, electron transport chain, oxidative phosphorylation and mitochondrial-associated energy metabolism, at both the transcriptomic and proteomic levels (Fig. 5b). Genes associated with PD, Huntington’s disease (HD), AD, aging and synaptic vesicle pathways were also downregulated. Downregulation of similar pathways was also observed in the pons (Fig. 5b). Comparison of DE proteins in frontal lobe and pons (P < 0.05, MSstatsTMT31) to the turquoise module identified in consensus WGCNA in Fig. 2a revealed significant overlap (P = 5.57 × 10−20, OR = 5.09 for frontal lobe; P = 1.21 × 10−22, OR = 5.70 for pons; GeneOverlap34; Fig. 5c). DE proteins in the frontal lobe also had significant overlap with the greenyellow module (P = 1.03 × 10−2, OR = 2.81, GeneOverlap34; Fig. 5c). Similar downregulation of genes with top kME values within the turquoise module was also observed in bulk tissue RNA and proteomics analyses (Fig. 5d).

Consistent with transcriptomic findings, at the protein level, EWCE38 results showed that there was a downregulation of deep-layer excitatory neuronal markers in the frontal lobe and pons (FDR-adjusted P = 1.5 × 10−4 and P < 1.00 × 10−5), upregulation of oligodendrocyte markers in both regions (FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5) and upregulation of BBB/endothelial cell markers in the frontal lobe (FDR-adjusted P = 1.30 × 10−2) (Fig. 6a). To expand upon these findings and evaluate additional brain regions, we conducted proteomic profiling of the hippocampus, temporal lobe, occipital lobe, basal ganglia, midbrain and thalamus (Fig. 6a and Supplementary Figs. 4 and 5). EWCE38 analyses revealed a widespread downregulation of excitatory neuronal markers in the hippocampus, occipital lobe, midbrain and thalamus, whereas the consistency of these findings was somewhat lower in the temporal lobe and basal ganglia (all FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5; Fig. 6a). Among these regions, hippocampus had the most significant upregulation of BBB/endothelial cells (FDR-adjusted P < 1.00 × 10−5), suggesting the presence of neuroinflammation62.

To further explore biological pathways across all eight brain regions, GO function terms shared across multiple regions were displayed in a network plot with similar terms forming clusters (Methods and Fig. 6b,c). Notably, mitochondrial function and neurodegenerative processes (cluster magenta, including PD, AD, HD, prion disease and TCA cycle/respiratory chain transport) were downregulated across multiple regions (Fig. 6b inset). mRNA processing, Rho GTPase signaling, ROBO receptor signaling and actin filament-based and microtubule-based processes were the shared upregulated pathways (Fig. 6c). Most of these pathways were shared by more than three regions, consistent with multi-regional alterations (Extended Data Fig. 5a; detailed shared functional terms are listed in Supplementary Tables 3 and 4). These results confirm the presence of cortical and subcortical alterations in multiple regions at the protein level and demonstrate strong overlap with multiple neurodegenerative disease-related changes. Merging the PPI networks from these eight regions (physical network, P < 0.001, STRING43) showed also similar enrichment within prominent synaptic, electron transport and TCA cycle deficits (Extended Data Fig. 5b). Together, proteomic profiling validates and expands the biological processes implicated by spatial transcriptomics.

Substantia nigra molecular changes parallel those in PD

Because many of the changes in gray matter overlapped with mitochondrial function and other pathways strongly implicated in PD pathogenesis, we further characterized changes in gene expression in the midbrain, where the loss of dopaminergic (DA) neurons in the substantia nigra is a neuropathological hallmark for PD63. Consistent with previous observations64,65, patients with COVID-19 in our cohort did not have significant pigmentation loss in DA neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta (representative histology shown in Fig. 7a,c), and midbrain nCounter direct gene expression analysis did not reveal significant DE genes (12 controls and 17 patients; Extended Data Fig. 1a).

Owing to the midbrain’s anatomical complexity, we next characterized the molecular changes in the midbrain using spatial transcriptomics, which allowed us to specifically select the substantia nigra, cerebral peduncle, periaqueductal gray matter and red nucleus (Fig. 7a) in the non-COVID controls and the patients with COVID-19 (n = 4 each; Supplementary Fig. 4c). We found a slightly elevated expression of ORF1ab in the periaqueductal gray matter, ACE2 in cerebral peduncle and NRP1 in substantia nigra and red nucleus, along with increased expression of TMPRSS11A and TMPRSS2 in substantia nigra, cerebral peduncle and red nucleus (Fig.7b). We also observed upregulation of IFNRA2 and IFITM1 and yet a significant downregulation of IFITM3 in the substantia nigra (Fig. 7b). These results indicated a very low-level presence of SARS-CoV2 and elevated expression of SARS-CoV2-related genes, similar to the pattern observed in the frontal cortex and pons.

In the substantia nigra, genes related to oxidative phosphorylation (ATP5PO, COX4I1 and NDUF4A), synaptic vesicle function (CALM3, KIF3C, SV2B and OAZ1), PD (PINK1 and PARK7) and hub genes of the turquoise consensus module from frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei (HSP90AB1 and LDHA) were downregulated (Fig. 7d). Genes related to metallothionein proteins (MT1F and MT1G), upregulated in the gray matter of frontal lobe and pons, were upregulated in substantia nigra (Fig. 7d; all BH-adjusted P < 0.005, LMM, Dream66). Further analyses of 90 oxidative phosphorylation genes (Methods) in both substantia nigra and red nucleus showed predominant downregulation, consistent with decreased mitochondrial function (Fig. 7e). These results indicate that neurons in various brain areas show similar vulnerability, suggesting a mechanism parallel to PD pathophysiology and brain aging.

No alterations of fibrin deposition in acute COVID-19

Although alterations at mRNA and protein levels indicated BBB upregulation with oxidative stress and pathogen response (Fig. 2e,g), H&E staining showed no histological evidence of BBB breakdown (Supplementary Table 1). Increased fibrin signals by IHC in both intravascular and perivascular areas, indicating BBB leakage67, were observed in two of five non-COVID controls and in one of four patients with COVID-19, with no significant difference (Extended Data Fig. 3b; P = 2.68 × 10−1, one-tailed unpaired t-test). This finding aligns with transcriptomic and proteomic analyses, suggesting that BBB inflammation, rather than breakdown, is a characteristic of severe acute/subacute COVID-19.

Minimal overlap with hypoxia-related changes

Patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19 often have acute respiratory failure, hypoxemia and various pulmonary pathological changes. Recognizing the potential confounding effect of hypoxia, we intentionally incorporated individuals with pulmonary pathology as non-COVID controls (Methods and Supplementary Table 1). To further investigate the extent to which hypoxia contributed to the transcriptomic alterations observed in patients with COVID-19, we conducted GSEA60,61 (Methods) of the spatial transcriptomic results across 10 areas in the frontal lobe, midbrain and pons, using hallmark gene sets containing classic hypoxia-induced genes. Extended Data Fig. 6a illustrates that hypoxia was not significantly enriched in any of the profiled areas. When comparing the differentially upregulated and downregulated genes of primary cortical neurons under hypoxic conditions68 with the top 500 DE genes in the gray matter (frontal gray matter, pontine nuclei and substantia nigra, BH-adjusted P < 0.05), no significant overlap was observed (Extended Data Fig. 6b; all P > 0.17, GeneOverlap34). We also compared the 500 upregulated genes in mouse brains under hypoxic conditions for 2 d and 7 d69 to those in the frontal gray matter and found no significant overlap (Extended Data Fig. 6b; P = 4.70 × 10−1 and 1.88 × 10−1, GeneOverlap34). In addition, within the non-COVID controls of nCounter bulk RNA direct gene expression assay for frontal lobe, differential expression analysis between individuals with no significant pulmonary pathology (n = 3) and those with various pulmonary pathology (n = 9) revealed no DE genes (Supplementary Data 4; DESeq2 (ref. 32)). These results indicate that hypoxia or pulmonary pathology is not the main driver of the molecular alterations that we observed in the patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19.

Increased amyloid deposition, but not Tau, in acute COVID-19

Because we observed a significant overlap of COVID-19 with neurodegenerative diseases in our multi-omic analyses, we investigated whether there were neurodegeneration-related pathological changes. Senile amyloid plaques, cerebral amyloid angiopathy and intracellular phosphorylated Tau accumulation as neurofibrillary tangles, neuropil threads and neuritic plaques are core neuropathological hallmarks defining AD70. As shown in Extended Data Fig. 7, three out of nine patients with COVID-19 have frequent senile plaques in the frontal cortex, and one patient has cerebral amyloid angiopathy with no senile plaques. Notably, no significant amyloid deposition was present in eight non-COVID controls (P = 4.61 × 10−2, unpaired t-test, one-tailed). Anti-phospho-Tau IHC did not show significant positive signals in either non-COVID controls or patients with COVID-19 (P = 4.84 × 10−1, unpaired t-test, one-tailed). Very rare neurons with neurofibrillary tangles and sparse neuropil threads were present in both groups. The elevated amyloid deposition, rather than Tau, suggests that COVID-19 involves unique pathological processes that partially overlap with neurodegenerative disorders and may potentially trigger neurodegeneration.

Discussion

Many patients with SARS-CoV2 infection or those post-infection exhibit neurological and/or psychiatric symptoms. Most studies have concentrated on molecular profiling of brain tissue in the frontal lobe of patients with COVID-19, leaving the extent of molecular changes across different brain regions largely unknown. Additionally, many of these studies involved small sample sizes, used only a single molecular profiling method or had limited spatial resolution. Furthermore, potential confounding factors, such as pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes, were not often adequately accounted for. To address these limitations, we conducted a multi-regional, multi-omic analyses of brain tissue in patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19, including spatial transcriptomics to achieve improved resolution. We focused on brain tissue with minimal histological changes to identify molecular changes not related to direct tissue damage, and controlled the pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal injury as common confounding factors in both control and patient groups. Although we could not completely exclude the effects of generalized hypoxia in our cohort, our results showed that hypoxia and/or pulmonary pathology were not the main drivers for the molecular changes that we observed.

Detection of SARS-CoV2 in postmortem brain has been inconsistent20,25,71. We found no viral mRNA or protein in bulk tissue profiling, but we identified low levels of viral mRNA using more sensitive spatial transcriptomics. However, it is still controversial whether the presence of viral mRNA indicates active infection72. The observed increased expression of SARS-CoV2-related genes—ACE2, FURIN and NRP1—at both mRNA and protein levels indicates the potential elevation of the central nervous system (CNS) cellular response to infection both inside and outside of the CNS. Consistent with their reported expression in pericytes and endothelial cells73,74, we observed increased ACE2 and FURIN expression around the vascular/perivascular structures. Consistent with snRNA-seq studies showing NPR1 expression in astrocytes, deep-layer neurons and endothelial cells40,75,76, we observed increased NRP1 immunoreactivity in those cells in patients with COVID-19. Interestingly, in addition to their roles involving SARS-CoV2 viral docking and processing, ACE2 and NRP1 have been implicated in various neurodegenerative conditions, such as AD, familial British dementia and Charcot–Marie–Tooth disease77-80. Similarly, IFITMs, particularly IFITM3, are elevated in patients with AD, aging mouse models81 and brain tumor metastasis82. Moreover, we did not directly observe an increase in BBB breakdown as previously reported83 but, rather, evidence of immune-inflammatory changes. We interpret our results, in conjunction with previous studies, that the extensive molecular changes we observed throughout the brain, which are out of proportion to the low viral load detected, are not solely attributable to direct CNS viral infection. Instead, they likely reflect a multifaceted response of the brain to the pronounced SARS-CoV2 infection in the periphery, involving immune response, dysfunction of the BBB and impairment of the CNS.

By studying multiple brain regions and using WGCNA, we identified consistent cell-type-specific changes indicating significant mitochondrial and synaptic defects in patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19. We found a robust, shared downregulation of neuronal function, especially excitatory neurons, across all cortical layers and subcortical regions. Deep-layer neurons in the frontal lobe were more impacted at both the mRNA and protein levels. Deep-layer pyramidal neurons in L5 and L6 are major excitatory output neurons from the neocortex84. They can be classified as intratelencephalic with projections within the telencephalon or extratelencephalic with broader projections84. Our results suggest that intratelencephalic neurons at L5 and L6 are particularly affected in patients with COVID-19.

More remarkably, we found that downregulated genes in patients with severe acute/subacute COVID-19 overlap significantly with those well-recognized components in multiple neurodegenerative diseases, such as PD85-87, AD88-90 and HD91,92, and aging93,94, emphasizing mitochondrial and synaptic dysfunction. Previous studies linked SARS-CoV2 infection in patients with acute and long-term COVID-19 with aging and neurodegeneration, based on genetic risk factors95,96, clinical symptom similarities2,97-99 and animal models100. Mitochondrial dysfunction has been implicated in organ damage outside the CNS, including heart failure101, leukocyte cell death102 and alveolar macrophage dysfunction103, in acute COVID-19 and post-acute sequelae of COVID-19 (PASC), or long COVID104. SARS-CoV2 proteins have been predicted to interact with multiple cellular compartments, including mitochondria105. Boosting mitochondrial fitness by inhibiting mitochondrial pyruvate carrier facilitates tissue recovery in the lung103. Our findings, which demonstrate significant mitochondrial defects in both cortical and subcortical regions, underscore the mechanistic link between SARS-CoV2 infection and increased susceptibility to neurodegeneration, thereby highlighting potential therapeutic avenues to slowing the brain-related disease’s trajectory in those experiencing both acute and long COVID.

Our observation of increased Aβ42-immunoreactive senile plaque and cerebral amyloid angiopathy, but not Tau-immunoreactive neurofibrillary tangles in patients with COVID-19, are consistent with the work of Priemer et al.106. Several in vitro studies have shown that the proteome of the SARS-CoV-2 virus contains peptide sequences exhibiting amyloidogenic properties107-109. In addition, amyloid depositions are seen in disease contexts other than dementia, such as traumatic brain injury110. Amyloid β peptides have been indicated to act as an antimicrobial peptide (AMP) that display a protective innate immune response to viral infections, including herpes virus111, and bacterial and fungal infections112,113. It is possible that amyloid deposition in patients with acute/subacute COVID-19 is a component of the general brain response to infection, potentially serving as an initial trigger for neurodegeneration.

Neuroinflammation has also been proposed as a mechanistic link between SARS-CoV2 infection and neurodegeneration23-26,95,98-100. Indeed, our much larger dataset across multiple brain regions with both transcriptomic and proteomic analyses revealed upregulation of glial cells, BBB/endothelial cells associating with pathways including metallothionein proteins, the NABA matrisome, oxidative stress and pathogen and immune response. These pathways are also upregulated in patients with COVID-19 in lung, urine and peripheral blood114-121. Here we highlight their involvement in the brain, alongside broad excitatory neuron involvement, providing more insights into COVID-19 pathogenesis.

Our study also highlights the effectiveness of using medically archived formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) autopsy brains for in-depth molecular profiling in diseases involving brains. Despite varied postmortem intervals, ventilation status and immunocompromised status in our samples, we accounted for these factors and confirmed the generalizability of our results. The inflammatory changes observed align with previous studies in the frontal lobe25,26,62 and tissues outside the CNS71,83, supporting our findings.

Several limitations of this work should be emphasized. First, larger sample sizes are needed to further explore the clinicopathological correlation. To further pinpoint the cellular mechanism of how different cell types contribute to the pathology in COVID-19, characterization of the molecular changes at single-cell level with retained spatial information should be conducted. Additionally, because postmortem studies are inherently limited to addressing causal relationships in disease onset and progression, further experiments in in vivo and in vitro settings are needed to establish causality and explore the effects of experimental perturbation. Our work serves as a starting point for such future investigations. Furthermore, additional work is needed to determine the specificity of our findings to the brain. Using Qiagen Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA)122, we compared the previously published DE proteins related to mitochondrial function from lung, adrenal gland, lymph node, basal ganglia, medulla, renal cortex, nasopharyngeal tissue121,123-125 and COVID-associated monocytic encephalitis (CAME) brain62 to those in our study and found inconsistent results across comparisons (Supplementary Fig.6). Notably, the same organ in different external studies exhibited different alterations in the same pathways. The inconsistencies and variability across studies are likely due to differences in disease stage, tissue sampling location, extent of tissue damage and control selection, which need to be controlled for in future studies.

In conclusion, our work suggests that mitochondrial and synaptic dysfunction in excitatory neurons across both cortical and subcortical regions, coupled with glial inflammatory processes, are critical mechanisms underlying CNS impairment after SARS-CoV-2 infection, potentially triggering the neurodegenerative processes. However, the incomplete overlap of the pathways dysregulated in COVID-19 with neurodegenerative diseases suggests an underlying unique disease process in COVID-19. In this context, our work provides a valuable reference resource for developing robust pathophysiological models and therapeutic approaches.

MethodsCOVID-19 patient and control cohorts

The patient group comprised 20 patients who had been diagnosed with COVID-19 during 2020–2021 either through antemortem or postmortem SARS-CoV2 viral RNA PCR from nasopharyngeal or bronchial swabs. None of the patients had received a COVID-19 vaccine. For the control group, 22 individuals who either tested negative for SARS-CoV2 of postmortem swabs from 2020–2021 or who had passed away in 2018–2019 were selected. Individuals with a history of organ transplants (lung, heart, kidney and bone marrow), as well as malignancy outside the CNS, were classified as immunocompromised. Individuals with diagnoses of neurodegenerative diseases, such as AD or PD, multiple sclerosis, primary brain malignancy and metastatic disease in the brain, were excluded. Because most of the patients with COVID-19 diagnoses had a history of mechanical ventilation, we balanced the control groups with individuals who had a history of mechanical ventilation. The clinical characteristics of the cohorts and histological findings of the brain and lung are listed in Supplementary Tables 1 and 2. The study was approved by the autopsy service at the Department of Pathology and Laboratory Medicine, David Geffen School of Medicine, University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA). All individuals included in the study were consented for research.

Histological tissue procurement.

Brain tissue sections (1–3 cm in greatest dimension) from designated areas were procured after proper fixation in formalin at room temperature for approximately 2 weeks. The designated areas included the frontal lobe, specifically the left watershed area between the superior and middle frontal gyri, as well as the superior pons, superior and middle temporal gyri, basal ganglia (comprising the internal capsule, globus pallidus and putamen), midbrain (at the level of red nucleus encompassing the substantia nigra and cerebral peduncle), occipital lobe (including the line of Gennari), hippocampus (comprising the dentate gyrus, CA1 to CA4 and subiculum at the level of the lateral geniculate nucleus) and thalamus at the level of the hippocampus. The tissue sections were embedded in paraffin and stored at room temperature.

IHC.

FFPE tissue sections were cut at 5–6-μm thickness. IHC analyses against ACE2, FURIN, NRP1, Fibrin and phosphorylated-Tau were conducted using a Leica Bond Rx auto-stainer with the Bond Polymer Refine Detection Kit (Leica Biosystems, DS9800). In brief, the sections underwent heat-induced antigen retrieval using either ER2 (BOND Epitope Retrieval Solution 2, Leica Biosystems, AR9640) or ER1 (BOND Epitope Retrieval Solution 1, Leica Biosystems, AR9961) buffer for 20 min, followed by a peroxide block for 5 min. After three brief washes with Bond wash buffer, the sections were incubated with primary antibodies: ACE2 (Proteintech, mouse monoclonal, cat. no. 66699-1, lot no. 10019610, ER1, dilution 1:1,000), NRP1 (Proteintech, mouse monoclonal, cat. no. 60067-1, lot no. 10026134, ER2, dilution 1:400), FURIN (Proteintech, rabbit polyclonal, cat. no. 18413-1-AP, lot no. 00120612, ER2, dilution 1:200), Fibrin (Millipore, mouse monoclonal, clone 59D8, cat. no. MABS2155, lot no. 3448042, ER2, dilution 1:200) and phosphor-Tau (Invitrogen, mouse monoclonal, cat. no. MN1020, lot no. Y14023268, ER2, dilution 1:500) for 30 min. Subsequently, the sections underwent three washes with BOND wash buffer before incubation with the post-primary antibodies from the BOND Polymer Refine Detection Kit for 10 min. After three 2-min washes with BOND wash buffer, the sections were incubated with polymer for 8 min, followed by five washes with BOND wash buffer. The slides were then washed with deionized water and incubated with Mixed DAB Refine, followed by another round of Mixed DAB Refine for 10 min. After three washes with deionized water, the sections were counterstained with hematoxylin. IHC against Aβ42 was performed manually. The sections were baked overnight at 60 °C and then deparaffinized and rehydrated using xylene and graded alcohol, respectively. Antigen retrieval was achieved by placing the slides in 70% formic acid for 10 min. After antigen retrieval, the sections were incubated at room temperature for 60 min with the primary antibody (anti-β-amyloid 1-42 antibody, EMD Millipore, rabbit polyclonal, AB5078P, lot no. 4041654, dilution 1:1,000). This was followed by incubation with ImmPRESS HRP horse anti-rabbit IgG, peroxidase (Vector Laboratories, cat. no. MP7401, lot no. ZL0314) for 40 min. Chromogen staining was achieved using N,N′-diaminobenzidine (Vector Laboratories, SK-4100), and counterstaining was performed with hematoxylin. Anti-Aβ42 and Tau IHCs were repeated once. The positive IHC signals were quantified by ImageJ126, followed by one-tailed unpaired t-test to compare the controls with the patients.

TMT labeling, CMMB-based isopropanol gradient peptide fractionation and liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry for proteomics of FFPE postmortem brain tissue.

(1) Sample preparation. For each region, 3–5 10-μm FFPE tissue scrolls were sectioned and deparaffinized, including three washes with xylene (5 min each), followed by two washes with 100% ethanol (5 min each), two washes with 95% ethanol (5 min each), one wash with 70% ethanol (5 min) and two washes with deionized water (5 min each). Tissue pellets were lysed in lysis buffer127 (0.3 M Tris pH 8.0, 50% acetonitrile) and incubated at 65 °C overnight. Later lysates were clarified by centrifugation at 16,100g for 15 min at 4 °C. Lysates were normalized by absorbance at 280 nm and dried for the following digestion. (2) Digestion. Each sample was started with 25 μg of dried protein pellet and resuspended in 50 μl of water, and, later, 150 μl of digestion buffer (8 M urea, 100 mM Tris pH 8.5) was added to each. Then, all the samples were reduced and alkylated via sequential 20-min incubations of 5 mM TCEP and 10 mM iodoacetamide at room temperature in the dark while being mixed at 1,200 r.p.m. in an Eppendorf thermomixer. Next, 10 μl of carboxylate-modified magnetic beads (CMMBs, also widely known as SP3 (ref. 128)) mixture was added to each sample. By increasing the ethanol concentration to over 50%, it allows the protein binding to CMMB. CMMB was washed three times with 80% ethanol and resuspended with 18 μl of 100 mM TEAB. Then, protein samples were digested overnight with 1 μl of 0.1 μg μl−1 LysC (Promega) and 2 μl of 0.4 μg μl−1 trypsin (Pierce) at 37 °C. (3) TMT labeling and CIF. First, 9 μl of 100% acetonitrile was added to each sample to achieve the final concentration to be 30% (v/v). The TMTpro 16plex (Thermo Fisher Scientific, A44522) was resuspended with 100% acetonitrile. Then, 25 μg of each sample was labeled with TMTpro labels. The labeled samples from each set were pooled to ensure equal and randomized labeling of both control and patient samples. Each set consisted of samples from the same designated brain region. The pooled samples were fractionated by the CIF method129 into six fractions before mass spectrometry analysis. (4) Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) acquisition and analysis. Fractionated samples were separated on a 75 μM ID × 25-cm C18 column packed with 1.9-μm C18 particles (Dr. Maisch) using a 140-min gradient of increasing acetonitrile and eluted directly into a Thermo Orbitrap Fusion Lumos mass spectrometer where MS spectra were acquired using synchronous precursor selection (SPS) MS3 technology130. Protein identification was performed using MaxQuant131 version 1.6.17.0. against the human reference proteome from EMBL (UP000005640_9606 HUMAN Homo sapiens, 20,874 entries). Searches were performed using a 20-p.p.m. precursor ion tolerance. TMTpro 16plex was set as a static modification on lysine and peptide N terminal. Carbamidomethylation of cysteine was set as static modification, and oxidation of methionine residues and N-terminal protein acetylation were set as variable modifications. LysC and trypsin were selected as enzyme specificity with maximum of two missed cleavages allowed. A 1% FDR was used as a filter at both protein and peptide-spectrum match (PSM) levels.

nCounter tissue RNA direct gene expression of FFPE postmortem brain tissue.

Tissue RNA from 3–4 sections (scrolls or slides), each measuring 10 μm, were extracted using a Qiagen FFPE RNA extraction kit (cat. no. 73504). Direct gene expression assays were performed using the nCounter XT assay27 at UCLA and Nanostring Technology according to the manufacturer’s protocol. In brief, a minimum of 100 ng of total RNA was used to hybridize with custom code-set. Then, 8 μl of master mix containing the hybrid buffer and the reporter probe was added to each of the 12 tubes and followed by 5 μl of 20 ng μl−1 RNA in each tube. Next, 2 μl of Capture ProbeSet was added to each tube immediately before placing at 65 °C and hybridize for at least 16 h. After hybridization, excess probes were washed away, and the purified target–probe complexes were eluted off from the magnetic beads and immobilized and aligned for data collection. Data collection was performed using epifluorescence microscopy and CCD capture technology on the nCounter analyzer to yield target molecule counts. Digital images were processed within the nCounter instrument, and the Reporter probe counts were tabulated in CSV format for data analysis. The mRNA probe targets comprised 825 genes associated with neuropathology, along with nine probes specific to SARS-CoV2.

GeoMx Spatial transcriptomics of FFPE postmortem brain tissue.

Tissue sections measuring 5–6 μm were mounted on glass slides (Super-Frost Plus, Fisherbrand, cat. no. 22-037-246 or Leica X-tra-adhesive, cat. no. 3800050) and were subjected to an overnight baking at 37 °C, followed by a 3-h baking at 65 °C. Each slide contains 1–3 tissue sections from different biological individuals. Sections from controls and patients were randomly selected and alternately placed on the slides. The slides underwent processing on the Leica automation platform (for frontal and pons) and manual processing (for midbrain), including antigen retrieval for 20 min at 100 °C and 0.25 μg ml−1 proteinase K treatment for 20 min. Subsequently, the slides were incubated overnight with the GeoMx Human whole transcriptome atlas (WTA)28 probe cocktail, targeting 18,676 genes (pool 1) and add-on panel (pool 2, three SARS-CoV2 targets with 23 genes included in pool 1). Immunofluorescent cell type/morphology markers, anti-NeuN antibody (Millipore, rabbit polyclonal, cat. no. ABN78, lot no. 3854475, dilution 1:100) and goat anti-rabbit IgG (H+L) secondary antibody, Alexa Fluor Plus 594 (Invitrogen, Thermo Fisher Scientific cat. no. A32740, lot no. XD344352, dilution 1:100) for neurons, anti-GFAP antibody with Alexa Fluor 488 (Invitrogen, mouse monoclonal, GA5 clone, cat. no. 53-9892-82, lot no. 2389127, dilution 1:400) for astrocyte, anti-IBA1 antibody with Alexa Fluor 647 (Millipore, mouse monoclonal, 20A12.1 clone, cat. no. MABN92-AF647, lot no. 3724623, dilution 1:100) for microglia and Syto83 (Invitrogen, cat. no. S11364, dilution 1:25,000) for nuclei were then applied to the slides. The GeoMx Deep Spatial Platform (DSP)28 was used to scan the slides, followed by ROI selections. The ROI (660.0 × 660.0 μm2) selections were performed by senior neuropathologists to ensure accurate identification of neuroanatomic structures in both patients with COVID-19 and controls. Probe barcodes were UV cleaved, collected and sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with paired-end reads. After sequencing, the processing and normalization of data involved matching the raw probe counts to sequencing results to filter out unused probes, remove outliers and third quartile Q3 normalization to the negative probes28. The negative probes include probes for whole transcriptomic atlas and SARS-CoV2 (Neg-WTA and SARS-CoV2-Neg).

Statistics and reproducibilitySample size, data distribution and differential expression analysis.

No statistical methods were used to pre-determine sample sizes; however, our sample sizes are similar to those reported in previous publications for postmortem human brain studies23-26,132,133. Although normal data distribution of the data was assumed, it was not formally tested. The investigators were not blinded to group allocation due to the need for careful selection of non-COVID controls to closely match the patients with COVID-19 in terms of age, gender, postmortem interval, immunocompromised status, disease duration, particularly ventilation status, pulmonary pathology and anoxic-ischemic neuronal changes. These are common confounding factors that are challenging to eliminate. In addition, tissue sections from both control and patient groups were examined under a microscope, and those without overt hemorrhage, infarction or infection were selected to minimize the influence of direct tissue damage.

For proteomics, we selected eight brain regions from 24 biologically independent individuals for profiling (Fig. 1a). A total of 126 samples were procured, including 16 samples (seven non-COVID controls and nine patients with COVID-19) from each of the following regions—frontal lobe, temporal lobe, occipital lobe, basal ganglia, midbrain, pons and hippocampus—as well as 14 samples (five non-COVID controls and nine patients with COVID-19) from the thalamus. After LC–MS, the statistical analysis including the differential expression analysis was conducted with MSstatsTMT31 version 2.10.0.

For tissue RNA direct gene expression assay, we selected four brain regions of 31 biologically independent individuals (13 non-COVID controls and 18 patients with COVID-19). Within each region, a separate sample ID was assigned to achieve randomization, regardless of COVID-19 diagnosis status. A total of 117 samples (31 from frontal lobe, 30 from basal ganglia, 27 from pons and 29 from midbrain) were profiled (Fig. 1a). For one patient with COVID-19, both left and right frontal lobe samples were procured owing to the tissue scarcity on the left side. Both samples passed the quality control and were kept for downstream analyses. After nCounter data collection, the raw counts underwent outlier removal (WGCNA134,135 version 1.70-3), normalization (RUVSeq136 version 1.36.0) and differential expression analysis (DESeq2 (ref. 32) version 1.34.0).

For spatial transcriptomics, we selected the tissue sections from a total of 15 biologically independent individuals (six non-COVID controls and nine patients with COVID-19), targeting areas with intact tissue integrity and minimal histopathological changes (Supplementary Tables 1 and 2). In the frontal lobe, sections were obtained from 11 individuals (five controls and six patients) at the left watershed area between the superior and middle frontal gyri and were divided into two areas: gray matter and white matter. The pons sections were taken from 10 individuals (four controls and six patients) at the superior pons and divided into four distinct areas: pontine nuclei, pontocerebellar fibers, corticospinal tracts and tegmentum. Midbrain sections were obtained from eight individuals (four controls and four patients) and divided into four distinct areas: the substantia nigra, cerebral peduncle, red nucleus and periaqueductal gray matter. A total of 319 ROIs (121 ROIs for the frontal gray and white matter, 119 ROIs for pons and 79 ROIs for midbrain) were selected and profiled. For the frontal and pons data, the differential expression analysis was performed using an LMM (lme4 (ref. 33) version 1.1-35.5) with the formula ~ Disease + (1∣ScanID) + (1∣LotID) along with Benjamini–Hochberg adjustment on the GeoMx Digital Spatial Profiler. For the midbrain data, sequencing results from different GeoMx DSP platforms (Nanostring Technology and UCLA TCGB) were combined and underwent outlier removal, linear regression to eliminate effects of batch and clinical traits (age, postmortem interval and ventilation status) and differential expression analysis using an LMM (Dream66) with the formula ~ Disease + (1∣ScanID). Ninety oxidative phosphorylation-related genes in Fig. 7e were selected from oxidative phosphorylation canonical pathway in Qiagen IPA122. Heatmap in Fig. 5d was generated using Morpheus (https://software.broadinstitute.org/morpheus). Venn diagrams were generated using https://molbiotools.com/listcompare.php.

EWCE analysis.

Expression matrix, metadata and/or Seurat objects of Wamsley et al.39, Yang et al.25 and Bakken et al.40 were downloaded. Cell type wrappers were generated using non-diseased controls to summarize expression specificity. Based on the classifications provided in the studies, the cell types were classified into two levels: major class cell types (level 1) and subclass cell types (level 2). Differentially upregulated and downregulated genes were selected based on their ranked values (−SIGN (log2(fold change)) × log10(P value)). A threshold of >2.5 for upregulation or <–2.5 for downregulation was applied for the spatial transcriptomic results. A threshold of >2.0 for upregulation or <–2.0 for downregulation was applied for frontal lobe bulk gene expression results. All DE proteins (P < 0.05) were used for proteomic results. EWCE (version 1.12.0) analysis was performed following the method described by Skene et al.38. The calculation of bootstrap enrichments was repeated for 100,000 randomly generated gene lists. The increase in cell type-specific enrichment, represented by the standard deviation from the mean (sd_from_mean) calculated using EWCE, was plotted, with sd_from_mean serving as a measure of significance38. Dot plots were generated using ProHits-viz137,138 (https://prohits-viz.org/).

Eight major cell types from Wamsley et al.39 were used (oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, BBB/endothelial cells, microglia, excitatory neurons, interneurons and oligodendrocyte progenitor cells) for analyzing DE genes from the six areas of the frontal lobe and pons, consensus modules and DE proteins. Thirty-six subclass cell types, including 19 subclass excitatory neurons, were used for analyzing DE genes in the frontal gray matter and frontal lobe bulk gene expression.

Furthermore, eight major cell types from Yang et al.25 (oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, endothelial cells, microglia, oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, excitatory neurons, NRGN neurons and inhibitory neurons) and 14 subclass cell types, including L2/3, L4, L5/6, L5/6 CC excitatory neurons, NRGN neurons and somatostatin, parvalbumin, vasointestinal peptide and SV2C interneurons, were used to analyze the enrichment of DE genes in the frontal gray and white matter, consensus modules and frontal lobe bulk gene expression.

Lastly, a total of 20 subclass cell types of Bakken et al.40 were used to analyze the enrichment of DE genes in the frontal gray and white matter, consensus module, and frontal lobe bulk gene expression.

Additionally, for classification of the NRGN neurons identified in Yang et al.25, NRGN neuron markers were chosen based on a specificity value exceeding 0.25 on a scale from 0 to 1 after generating the cell type wrapper. At major cell type classification (level 1), 2,503 genes were identified. At subclass cell type classification (level 2), 458 genes were identified. These NRGN neuron markers were used as inputs for EWCE analysis. NRGN level 1 markers were compared to the major eight cell types identified in Wamsley et al.39. NRGN level 2 markers were compared to 36 subclass cell types in Wamsley et al.39 and 20 subclass cell types in Bakken et al.40. As shown in Supplementary Fig. 1d, NRGN neuron represents a group of non-layer-specific neurons that has markers of both superficial and deep-layer excitatory neurons.

WGCNA.

The spatial transcriptomic results from frontal gray matter (62 ROIs) and pontine nuclei (37 ROIs) underwent outlier removal (95 ROIs remained) and linear regression to eliminate potential confounding factors. The regression formula used was as follows: formula ~ Disease + ScanID + LotID + Age + Gender + PMI + Duration + Ventilation + Immunocompromised + Sequencing PC1-5. The effects of ScanID, sequencing effects, Age, PMI and Ventilation were subtracted. Consensus network analysis was subsequently conducted using a step-by-step construction approach, following the methods described by Langfelder et al.134,135. WGCNA version 1.70-3 was used. In brief, topological overlap matrices (TOMs) were generated for the frontal gray matter and pontine nuclei, respectively, with a soft threshold of 12 for frontal gray matter and 24 for pontine nuclei. A consensus TOM was then calculated, serving as the input for hierarchical clustering and module detection. Disease-related significantly changed modules were selected through a linear model test of module eigengene values. Hub genes within each module were identified based on their kME values. Hub genes networks were generated using STRING43,139 version 11 and Cytoscape140 version 3.10.2 (Fig. 2). Volcano plots were generated using VolcaNoseR141 (https://goedhart.shinyapps.io/VolcaNoseR/).

High-dimensional WGCNA was conducted on the published data of Yang et al.25 using Seurat version 4.0.3 and hdWGCNA version 0.2.18 and by following the method described at https://smorabit.github.io/hdWGCNA/articles/basic_tutorial.html (refs. 29,30). In brief, the normalized expression matrix underwent linear regression to remove the confounding effects of covariates (percentage of mitochondrial RNA, nUMI/Count_RNA, nFeature_RNA, Age, Gender, Batch and nNuclei). The regressed expression matrix then underwent metacell groups generation, data normalization and scaling. Network construction was performed within specific cell type groups, excitatory neurons and NRGN neurons, respectively, with a soft threshold of 8. TOMs were calculated and served as inputs for module detections. Disease-related significantly changed modules were selected using the linear model (Fig. 3 and Supplementary Fig. 2).

Pathway and process enrichment/GO analysis.

DE genes in spatial transcriptomics, nCounter assay (BH-adjusted P < 0.1) and DE proteins from eight regions (P < 0.05) were submitted for GO analysis at Metascape42 version 3.5. The GO sources used for analysis included KEGG Pathway142, GO Biological Processes143,144, Reactome Gene Sets145, Canonical Pathways60, CORUM146 and WikiPathways147. P values were calculated based on the cumulative hypergeometric distribution for over-representation and the BH method for multi-test adjustment42. Shared gene plots across different areas were generated by Circos57 (Fig. 4a,d). Significant enrichment terms were hierarchically clustered with a 0.3 kappa score threshold. The GO term with the highest P value in each cluster is displayed42 (Fig. 4b,e). A network layout of representative enrichment terms is displayed with each term depicted as a circle node. The node size reflects the number of associated input genes, and color indicates cluster identity. Nodes with a similarity score above 0.3 are connected by edges42 (Fig. 6b,c). The enrichment network is also displayed with the color of each node proportionate to the number of gene hits of each term across all the regions42 (Fig. 6b insets and Extended Data Fig. 5a). Module genes of excitatory neurons and NRGN neurons from hdWGCNA were also submitted to EnrichR56 version 3.2 for functional analysis (Extended Data Fig. 4e and Supplementary Fig. 2c).

PPI enrichment analysis.

PPI analysis was conducted on the DE genes and proteins from each anatomical area individually as well as in combination, using Metascape version 3.5 (ref. 42) and STRING version 11 (refs. 43,139). Additional databases also included BioGrid version 4.4 (ref. 148), OmniPath149 and InWeb_IM150. Only physical interactions in STRING and BioGrid were selected. The Molecular Complex Detection (MCODE) algorithm58, which predicts protein interactions based solely on connectivity data, was employed to identify densely connected genes and proteins in each PPI network. This algorithm remains unaffected by the known high rate of false positives associated with data from high-throughput interaction techniques58. The MCODE networks further underwent pathway/GO enrichment analysis, and top terms were selected to label each network (Fig. 4c and Extended Data Fig. 5b).

GSEA.

The DE genes and proteins from each area and region were ranked and subjected to GSEA version 4.2.2 analysis (C2 curated gene sets and hallmark gene sets)60,61. Enriched gene sets that were shared between transcriptomics and proteomics were selected based on the BH-adjusted P (q) value and normalized enrichment score (NES) value. For the NES value, a threshold of >1.9 for upregulation and <–1.9 for downregulation was applied. An adjusted P value threshold of 0.025 for spatial transcriptomics results, 0.1 for the proteomics results of the frontal lobe and 0.5 for the proteomics results of the pons was used (Fig. 5b).
