Summary

Spatial transcriptomics (ST) data provide spatially informed gene expression profiles. However, power is limited for spatially informed differential gene expression (DGE) of complex diseases such as Alzheimer disease (AD), due to small sample sizes of ST data. Conversely, single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) data offer larger sample sizes for cell-type-specific (CTS) analyses but lack spatial information. Here, we integrated ST and snRNA-seq data to enhance the power of spatially informed CTS DGE analysis of AD-related phenotypes. We first utilized the CeLEry tool to infer six cortical layers of ∼1.5 million cells in the snRNA-seq data that were profiled from the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) tissue of 436 postmortem brains. Then, we conducted cortical layer- and cell-type-specific (LCS) and CTS DGE analyses based on the linear mixed model, for β-amyloid, tangle density, and cognitive decline. We identified 138 LCS significant genes with false discovery rate (FDR) q <0.05, including 103 for β-amyloid, 24 for tangle density, and 25 for cognitive decline. The majority of these LCS significant genes, including known AD risk genes such as APOE, KCNIP3, and CTSD, cannot be detected by CTS analyses. We also identified 2 genes shared across all 3 phenotypes and 10 shared between 2 phenotypes. Gene set enrichment analyses with the LCS DGE results of microglia in cortical layer 6 of β-amyloid identified 12 significant AD-related pathways. In conclusion, incorporating spatial information with snRNA-seq data enhanced the power of spatially informed DGE analyses. These identified LCS significant genes not only help illustrate the pathogenesis of AD but they also provide potential targets for developing therapeutics of AD.

Keywords: spatial transcriptomics, snRNA-seq, differential gene expression, Alzheimer disease, amyloid-beta, tangle density, cognitive decline

Spatial transcriptomics and snRNA-seq data were integrated to enhance spatially informed, cell-type-specific differential gene expression analysis in Alzheimer disease (AD). We identified 138 layer-specific AD-associated genes (e.g., APOE, KCNIP3), which are undetectable without spatial context. Pathway analyses revealed AD-relevant microglial signatures, highlighting the utility of spatial resolution in uncovering AD mechanisms and therapeutic targets.

Introduction

Spatial transcriptomics (ST) technologies have revolutionized the understanding of spatially informed gene expression, which provide invaluable insights into the molecular architecture of complex diseases such as Alzheimer disease (AD).1 Despite the additional spatial information, employing ST data to study complex diseases still faces significant challenges. Most ST studies of the human brain involve small sample sizes (n < 10).2 In particular, human brain ST data for studying AD are significantly limited, compared to available single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) datasets. Additionally, most available human brain ST data were profiled from healthy subjects, which further limits their application in disease-focused studies.

Besides limited sample sizes, another drawback of spatial ST data compared to snRNA-seq data is the trade-off between resolution and gene coverage. Sequencing-based ST can profile the whole transcriptome but only at spot-bulk resolution, while in situ-based ST techniques can only profile <500 genes at single-cell resolution. These limitations severely restrict the possibility of conducting spatially informed and cell-type-specific (CTS) differential gene expression (DGE) analyses of complex diseases.

In contrast to ST, snRNA-seq data of transcriptome-wide genes can be profiled at a much lower cost and with much larger sample sizes.3,4,5,6 Although snRNA-seq data allow CTS DGE analyses, snRNA-seq data lack the spatial contexts of all single nucleus/cells that are critical for fully understanding the cellular and molecular complexity of AD. The spatial arrangement of cells within tissues can reveal spatial specific interactions and functional connectivity that are vital for deciphering the pathogenesis of AD.7

To detect genes with spatially informed CTS differential expressions in AD-related phenotypes (β-amyloid, tangle density, and cognitive decline), we integrated public reference ST data provided by the Lieber Institute for Brain Development (LIBD)8 with snRNA-seq data of a large cohort3,4,5,6 to conduct cortical layer- and cell-type-specific (LCS) and CTS DGE analyses (Figure 1). The snRNA-seq data were profiled from the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) tissue of 436 postmortem brains in the Religious Order Study and the Rush Memory and Aging Project (ROS/MAP) cohorts.3,4,5,6 We first utilized a deep learning tool, Celery,9 to infer the spatial locations of six cortical layers for ∼1.5 million cells in the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data. We used the LIBD ST data as the reference data (Figure 1A). These six annotated cortical layers are characterized by distinct anatomical structures and biological functions. Next, we utilized the linear mixed model (LMM)-based testing method as implemented by genome-wide efficient mixed-model analysis (GEMMA)10 to test DGE for each gene-phenotype pair, using the pseudo-bulk scRNA-seq data of each cell type in each cortical layer (Figure 1B).

By integrating ST with snRNA-seq data, we identified 138 LCS significant genes, 109 of which were missed by CTS DGE analyses that do not consider the spatial information. Important AD-related significant pathways were only found enriched with the top LCS significant genes of microglia (Mic) in cortical layer 6 of β-amyloid. Additionally, we identified important known AD risk genes with differential expression only by LCS analyses—for example, APOE was found to be significant only in Mic in layers 5 and 6 for β-amyloid.

In the following sections, we describe the datasets used in this study, followed by the Celery method for annotating the spatial information of cells in the snRNA-seq data, the LMM model used for DGE analyses, and the gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) procedure. Then, we present the results and discuss the importance of integrating ST and snRNA-seq data for DGE analyses.

Material and methods

Data descriptions

LIBD data

The LIBD dataset comprises ST data profiled from 3 human subjects, including 2 males and 1 female, each with 4 replicates, resulting in a total of 12 sections. This dataset contains ST data for 33,538 genes and 47,681 spots, generated by using the Visium platform.8 For each subject, 2 spatially adjacent replicates are positioned without a gap, with 2 additional replicates located 300 μm away.

The cortical layers in the LIBD dataset have been manually labeled using the spatialLIBD tool,11 assigning each spot to one of the six cortical layers (L1–L6) and white matter (WM).12 L1, known as the molecular layer, consists of fiber and synapse-rich regions with few cell bodies, serving as a pathway for numerous parallel axons. L2, the external granular layer, mainly contains small pyramidal neurons with apical dendrites directed toward the brain’s surface, playing key roles in cortical connectivity. L3, the external pyramidal layer, features a thick arrangement of medium-sized pyramidal neurons that connect extensively within and across brain hemispheres. L4, the internal granular layer, is densely packed with stellate and small pyramidal cells, critical for receiving and processing sensory information. L5, the internal pyramidal layer, contains medium-to-large pyramidal neurons, including Betz cells, which project to subcortical regions influencing motor control. L6, the multiform layer, comprises a diverse mix of neuron types, primarily facilitating communication with the thalamus.12 Both reference LIBD ST data and ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data were profiled from the same DLPFC region. This LIBD ST dataset was used as a reference for training a deep learning model to learn the relationship between spatial location and gene expression profile. The trained model will then be used for inferring cortical layer locations of nuclei in the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data.

CosMx ST data

The CosMx dataset, characterized by a pre-commercial version of the 6078-plex CosMx Spatial Molecular Imager (SMI), is a single-cell resolution ST dataset profiled from human frontal cortex formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded tissue. Given its single-cell resolution and its origin from the same DLPFC region as the LIBD data and ROS/MAP snRNAsesq data, we utilized the CosMx data to guide our selection of the best reference ST from 12 samples in the LIBD dataset.

To annotate cortical layers of single cells in the CosMax ST data, we used the unsupervised clustering method SpaGCN13 to divide the ST brain section into distinct domains that closely reflect cortical layer structures. We then assigned each unidentified domain to one of the six cortical layers or WM by adopting the annotation strategy from the spatialLIBD tool. First, we compiled lists of marker genes for the six cortical layers and WM based on previous studies. Second, we conducted two-sample t tests for each marker gene to compare its expression in an unidentified domain against the remaining domains. Each unidentified domain is represented by a vector of t-statistics across marker genes. Third, we calculated the correlations between these t-statistic vectors from unidentified domains and corresponding vectors from cortical layers in the LIBD data. Last, we assigned each unidentified domain to the cortical layer or WM with the highest correlation.

ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data

The ROS/MAP dataset originates from two longitudinal, community-based clinic pathological cohort studies: ROS and MAP.3,4,5,6 Participants were recruited without known dementia at the time of enrollment and agreed to annual clinical evaluations and brain donation upon death. Each study received approval from the institutional review board (IRB) of Rush University Medical Center, with all participants signing informed consent forms, Anatomical Gift Act agreements, and repository consents. Collectively referred to as ROS/MAP, these harmonized cohorts are designed to be analyzed jointly. In the present study, the snRNA-seq data were profiled from 436 postmortem brain specimens of DLPFC tissue from the ROS/MAP cohorts, yielding transcriptomic profiles of 1,546,794 cells3,4,5,6 Our analyses focused on six major cell types: astrocytes (Ast), excitatory neurons (Ex), inhibitory neurons (In), Mic, oligodendrocytes (Oli), and Oli precursor cells (OPC).

Ethics approval and consent to participate

All ROS/MAP participants enrolled without known dementia and agreed to detailed clinical evaluation and brain donation at death (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29865057/). Both studies were approved by an IRB of Rush University Medical Center (ROS IRB no. L91020181, MAP IRB no. L86121802). Both studies were conducted according to the principles expressed in the Declaration of Helsinki. Each participant signed an informed consent, Anatomic Gift Act, and Rush Alzheimer’s Disease Center repository consent (IRB no. L99032481) allowing his or her data and biospecimens to be repurposed. ROS/MAP transcriptomics data were shared with a data use agreement.

AD-related phenotypes

Our study concentrated on three quantitative AD-related phenotypes: cognitive decline and two AD neuropathologic changes traits (tangle density and β-amyloid). Cognitive decline was measured as the person-specific rate of change in global cognition, estimated as person-specific random effects over all follow-up periods from an LMM.14,15 Tangle density was determined using molecularly specific immunohistochemistry, represented as the average PHFtau tangle density across 2 or more 20-μm sections from 8 brain regions: hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, midfrontal cortex, inferior temporal cortex, angular gyrus, calcarine cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and superior frontal cortex. Similarly, β-amyloid was quantified as the average percentage of cortical area occupied by β-amyloid protein in adjacent sections from the same 8 brain regions. To enhance normality, the values of tangle density and β-amyloid were transformed by taking their square root.16,17

Infer cortical layers for ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data by Celery

We employed the recently developed tool Celery9 to infer the spatial locations of ∼1.5 million cells from the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data. Celery is a supervised deep learning tool designed to recover the spatial locations of cells in snRNA-seq data. Celery trains a deep learning model to capture the relationship between gene expression and spatial locations by using a reference ST dataset and then infers the spatial locations of additional cells by using their transcriptomic profiles generated by snRNA-seq. Compared with alternative spatial reconstruction tools, such as novoSpaRc,18 SpaOTsc,19 and Tangram,20 Celery has demonstrated superior performance, achieving higher accuracy in both top-1 (the percentage of spots correctly assigned to their exact cortical layer) and top-2 accuracy (the percentage of spots assigned either to the correct cortical layer or an adjacent layer).9 Furthermore, Celery uniquely allows integration of multiple ST datasets into a unified reference framework, a critical feature not available in other methods. Therefore, we used Celery for cortical layer reconstruction in this study.

We utilized LIBD ST data from 8 out of 12 sections of the DLPFC as the reference ST data for training the Celery model. We selected these 8 sections because they encompassed the data of all 6 cortical layers and WM. Specifically, sections with IDs 151673, 151674, 151675, and 151676 originated from the same brain sample. Sections 151507, 151508, 151509, and 151510 originated from another brain. Since Celery can utilize ST reference data profiled from either one or multiple sections, we conducted experiments to select the best reference for inferring the spatial location of cells in the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq dataset. We considered various strategies by using only a single section, multiple sections from the same brain, and multiple sections from both brains as the reference data.

To evaluate the accuracy of inferred spatial domains, we compared the cell-type proportions in inferred cortical layers based on different reference ST data, to the gold standard provided by the single-cell resolution CosMx ST data. We used Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence21 to measure the similarity between the matrix of cell-type proportions in all six cortical layers and WM inferred by Celery and the corresponding matrix based on the single-cell resolution CosMx ST data. KL divergence, denoted as Dkl(P‖Q), is a type of statistical distance, measuring how one probability distribution P is different from a second reference probability distribution Q.

Here, the probability distribution P(x) refers to the cell-type proportions for each inferred cortical layer or WM summing up to 1, and the probability distribution Q(x) represents the cell-type proportions for each annotated cortical layer or WM of the single-cell resolution CosMx ST data. Since P(x) and Q(x) represent columns in the comparing matrices, the KL divergence as calculated by Equation 1 is appropriate for comparing the distance between two matrices that have cell-type proportions in all six cortical layers and WM in the columns:

Dkl(P|Q)=∑x∈χP(x)log(P(x)Q(x)) (Equation 1)

The reference ST data giving the cell-type proportion matrix with the smallest KL divergence to the one based on CosMx ST data will be selected as the optimal ST reference. Our LCS DGE analyses were conducted based on the inferred cortical layers based on this selected optimal ST reference dataset.

LCS DGE analysis by GEMMA

With the inferred spatial locations for all cells in the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data, we next conducted LCS DGE analyses for three quantitative AD-related phenotypes, β-amyloid, tangle density, and cognitive decline. For each gene of each sample, we quantified pseudo-bulk read counts of each cell type in each cortical layer. Genes with average pseudo-bulk read counts less than five were excluded from the downstream DGE analysis. Pseudo-bulk read counts were normalized and converted to read counts per million base pairs (CPM). The log2 transformed CPM data were used to conduct LCS DGE analysis.

To control for potential false positive inflations,22 we utilized the LMM approach implemented in the GEMMA tool.10 Confounding covariates including study, age at death, sex, and postmortem interval were adjusted for in the LMM. To test whether gene j with expression levels Xj,k,l of cell type k in cortical layer l is differentially expressed with respect to quantitative trait Yn×1 (H0:βj,k,l=0), the following LMM is assumed:

Y=Wα+Xj,k,lβj,k,l+Zu+ε;j=1,…,J;k=1,…,K;l=1,…,L; (Equation 2)
u∼MVN(0,γτ−1M);ε∼MVN(0,τ−1In)

Here, βj,k,l is the effect size of gene j in cell type k and cortical layer l; W is a n×c matrix of confounding covariates and an intercept term; α is a c×1 vector of covariate effects; Z is a n×n loading matrix, which is taken as an identity matrix22; ε is an n×1 vector of independent errors following a normal distribution with mean 0 and variance τ−1; u is an n×1 vector denoting random effects of all n samples following a multivariate normal distribution, with mean 0 and variance-covariance matrix γτ−1M; γ is the ratio of variance components between the random effects and error term; M is an n×n sample-sample correlation matrix derived from transcriptome-wide gene expression profiles; and In is an n×n identity matrix.

For comparison, we also conducted a CTS DGE analysis by using the same LMM as in Equation 2, and pseudo-bulk read counts obtained for each cell type per gene per sample. That is, for each gene j, we tested whether its expression levels Xj,k in cell type k is differentially expressed with respect to Yn×1 (H0:βj,k=0).

GSEA

Preranked GSEA as implemented in the fgsea23 package in R makes it possible to identify pathways that contain many co-regulated genes from a priori defined gene sets.23 The preranked GSEA method enhances the sensitivity and accuracy of pathway enrichment analyses in transcriptional datasets. For each phenotype, we conducted GSEAs by fgsea with the top 500 significant genes from two pairs of cell type and cortical layer, which have the most LCS significant genes.

We utilized multiple curated gene set databases from the human Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB)24: hallmark gene sets (H), curated gene sets (C2), ontology gene sets (C5), and cell-type signature gene sets (C8). The H gene sets summarize specific biological states or processes through coherent expression patterns, crafted by identifying overlaps across gene sets and retaining coordinately expressed genes. The C2 gene sets are assembled from online pathway databases, biomedical literature, and inputs from domain experts. This collection includes gene sets categorized under chemical and genetic perturbations and canonical pathways. C5 gene sets contain genes annotated by the same ontology term, with subcollections from Gene Ontology (GO) that cover biological processes, cellular components, and molecular functions, as well as from the human phenotype ontology. C8 gene sets were derived from single-cell sequencing studies. These gene sets represent cluster marker genes for various cell types across multiple human tissues and are used to aid cell-type assignment in complex datasets, such as those derived from organoid models.

Results

Accurate cortical layers were inferred for cells with snRNA-seq profiles

Inferred cortical layers were obtained for ∼1.5 million cells in the ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data of DLPFC, by using the Celery tool with LIBD ST reference data (see material and methods). The numbers and proportions of cells inferred to each cortical layer and WM are presented in Table S1. The proportions of cells inferred to each cortical layer and WM for each cell type are plotted in the heatmaps in Figure S1 and the bar plots in Figure S2. We assessed the accuracy of predicted cell-type proportions in cortical layers and WM by comparing their KL divergences to the cell-type proportions in the CosMx ST data profiled from the same DLPFC tissue (see material and methods). The minimum KL divergence was observed for the inference results based on the reference ST data from sample no. 151673 (Figure 2).

From the cortical layers inferred based on reference sample 151673 (Figure S1), we found that cortical layer 3 contained most cells (26.08%) compared to other cortical layers. Also, from the right panel in Figure 2, we found that Ast is the major cell type in cortical layer 1 with proportion 55%; Ex was the major cell type in cortical layers 2–5; Oli was the major cell type in cortical layers 6 (53%). These cell-type proportions in the inferred cortical layers and WH are similar to the ones obtained from the single-cell resolution CosMx ST data (left panel in Figure 2) and are consistent with the real application results of DLPFC tissue in the Celery paper.9

These inferred locations of cortical layers 1–6 were used in the follow-up LCS DGE analyses. Since cells inferred to the WM are primarily Oli (81%), the number of cells is limited for studying other cell types in WM. Also, the results are expected to be similar to the ones obtained by CTS DGE analyses of Oli. Cells inferred to the WM layer were excluded in our LCS DGE analyses.

We also evaluated the robustness of our LCS DGE analyses by using inferred spatial locations from sample 151674, a different section originating from the same brain specimen as the ST sample 151673. Taking the inferred cortical layer 5 of Mic cells as an example, we compared the LCS DGE effect sizes of all test genes for β-amyloid (Figure S3). The scatterplot results demonstrated strong consistency in gene effect sizes based on inferred cortical layers from these two reference sections, highlighting the robustness of Celery’s location inference.

LCS DGE analyses identified 138 significant genes

We conducted the LCS DGE analyses for three quantitative AD-related phenotypes: β-amyloid (n = 433), tangle density (n = 433), and cognitive decline (n = 436). By utilizing the LMM, we observed that false positive rates were well calibrated compared to the results with severely inflated false positives obtained by standard linear regression modeling (Figure S4).22 We identified a total of 138 LCS significant genes with false discovery rate (FDR) q < 0.05 across all cortical layers for each cell type and phenotype, including 103 for β-amyloid, 24 for tangle density, and 25 for cognitive decline (Figure 3A). To show the improved power by incorporating inferred spatial location information in the DGE analyses, we compared to the results obtained by standard CTS DGE analyses without considering spatial information. The standard CTS DGE analyses identified 38 significant genes with FDR q < 0.05, including 15 genes for β-amyloid, 16 genes for tangle density, and 14 genes for cognitive decline. As shown in Figure 3A, 90.3% of these CTS significant genes for β-amyloid, 62.5% for tangle density, and 78.6% for cognitive decline were also LCS significant genes. Because we failed to account for spatial information, 89 LCS significant genes in β-amyloid, 14 in tangle density, and 14 in cognitive decline were missed.

We present the distributions of significant LCS genes across six cell types in Figure 3B, and across six cortical layers in Figure 3C. For β-amyloid, the most significant LCS genes were observed in Mic (N = 78) and In (N = 41), predominantly in cortical layers 5 and 6. For tangle density, the number of significant LCS genes is predominant in Mic (N = 16) and oligodenrocytes (N = 11), and cognitive decline is predominant in Ex (N = 16) and Mic (N = 7). Compared to β-amyloid, significant LCS genes associated with tangle density and cognitive decline were more evenly distributed across all layers.

GSEAs identified AD-related pathways

For each phenotype, we conducted four sets of GSEAs, each with the selected top 500 significant genes in the LCS DGE results of the top 2 cortical layers and 2 cell types that have most significant genes (Table 1). For β-amyloid, the top two cell types were identified as Mic and In. Then, cortical layers 5 and 6 were identified as the top 2 cortical layers, and cortical layers 3 and 4 were identified for In. For tangle density, cortical layers 2, 3, and 4 in Mic and cortical layers 4 and 5 in Oli were identified. Given the similarity in cortical layers 3 and 4, we chose cortical layer 2 instead of cortical layer 3 to conduct the GSEA for Mic and tangle density. For cognitive decline, we selected cortical layers 2 and 3 in the Ex and Mic cell types.

We identified 12 significant pathways directly related to AD with FDR <0.05 (Figure 4A), which were enriched with LCS significant genes of β-amyloid in cortical layer 6 of Mic. Notably, ribosomal pathways such as the Ribosome from the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (FDR = 1.64E−15),25,26,27 Cytoplasmic Ribosomal Proteins from WikiPathways (FDR = 3.87E−13),28 and Ribosome from GO Cellular Component (FDR = 4.73E−14)29,30 were identified, underscoring the role of ribosome family proteins in AD pathogenesis.31

Additional significant AD-related pathways identified in the curated gene sets C2 include the following six pathways: (1) the eukaryotic translation initiation pathway from REACTOME (FDR = 1.64E−15)32 underscores the role of the translational machinery, particularly the α subunit of eukaryotic initiation factor-2, in AD pathogenesis.33 (2) Pathway signal recognition particle (SRP)-dependent cotranslational protein targeting to membrane from REACTOME (FDR = 1.89E−14) focuses on SRP-dependent cotranslational protein targeting to the membrane. Proteins in this pathway have been associated with periodontal disease and AD, indicating a broader impact on health.34 (3) Pathway metabolism of amino acids and derivatives from REACTOME (FDR = 1.64E−15)35,36,37 covers the metabolism of amino acids and their derivatives, noting significant changes in the levels of enzymes involved in amino acid metabolism observed in the AD brain.38 (4) Pathway nervous system development from REACTOME (FDR = 9.32E−9) focuses on the developmental processes of the nervous system.39,40,41 (5) Pathway of genes highly expressed in prenatal hippocampus from MODY (FDR = 1.12E−5) highlights genes highly expressed in the prenatal hippocampus, reflecting critical developmental molecular programs, which is a cortical structure critical for learning and memory.42 (6) Pathway about upregulated in the cerebellum of aged adult mice compared to young adults from LEE (FDR = 0.038) is a pathway that highlights how aging impacts cognitive and motor skills and is a risk factor for neurological disorders, including AD and Parkinson disease.43

One significant AD-related pathway, human embryonic midbrain neurotypes in the basal plate from MANNO, was identified in the C8 gene sets. This pathway pertains to the molecular diversity of midbrain development across mouse, human, and stem cell studies (FDR = 4.58E−3).44 Understanding the human embryonic ventral midbrain is crucial for research on Parkinson disease and may also intersect with AD mechanisms.

In addition, two significant AD pathways were identified in the H gene sets. One pathway was genes encoding proteins involved in glycolysis and gluconeogenesis (FDR = 0.02).24 Notably, reduced glucose metabolism is observed in patients at risk of AD, highlighting the relevance of this pathway.45 The second pathway is genes upregulated by STAT5 in response to interleukin-2 (IL-2) stimulation (FDR = 0.02),24 where IL-2 plays a dual role by enhancing memory formation and controlling inflammation, making it a promising target for AD research.46

With LCS DGE results of cognitive decline, four significant pathways were identified. Specifically, two AD-related pathways were found within the C8 gene sets in Mic in cortical layer 3: (1) fetal retinal genes in Mic from Hu et al.,47 with FDR = 0.0203. Previous studies demonstrate that retinal biomarkers can detect biological changes associated with AD and monitor disease progression over time48; and (2) differentially expressed genes in Mic from the prefrontal cortex identified by Zhong et al.,49 with FDR = 0.0203, highlighting genes critical to cognitive functions such as memory, cognitive ability, decision-making, and social behavior.

As a comparison, we also conducted GSEA by using the top 500 significant genes obtained by CTS DGE analyses for each phenotype. Significant AD-related pathways are presented in Figure S5. These significant pathways are not overlapped with those identified based on LCS DGE analysis results, highlighting that LCS DGE provided additional insights for studying AD-related phenotypes.

Genes identified by LCS DGE for multiple AD-related phenotypes

By comparing the LCS significant genes with differential expression for these 3 analyzed AD-related phenotypes, we found 2 significant genes (DPYD and PTPRG) shared by all 3 phenotypes, 4 genes (CREB5, CADM1, FOXP1, and IL15) shared by β-amyloid and tangle density, 1 gene (KCNIP3) shared by cognitive decline and tangle density, and 5 genes (NPNT, AGBL1, AL122014.1, HES4, and ALCAM) shared by β-amyloid and cognitive decline (Figure 4B; Table 2), Overall, a total of 12 unique LCS significant genes were identified in at least 2 of these AD-related phenotypes.

Several significant genes, including NPNT, DPYD, PTPRG, FOXP1, and IL15, were also found to be significant in at least two phenotypes by CTS DGE analyses (Table 2). Nonetheless, incorporating spatial location information allowed us to pinpoint the specific cortical layers where differential expression occurred. For example, IL15, a pro-inflammatory cytokine studied as a potential biomarker for AD,50 was significantly differentially expressed in Mic within cortical layer 2 for β-amyloid (FDR = 3.05E−2) and tangle density (FDR = 3.53E−2).

Importantly, 5 of these 12 genes would have been missed without considering spatial information. For instance, CREB5, a gene critical for synaptic plasticity, was significantly differentially expressed in Ast in cortical layer 3 for β-amyloid (FDR = 1.14E−2) and cortical layer 2 for tangle density (FDR = 2.26E−2). The CREB signaling pathway is essential for converting short-term memory into long-term memory and is implicated in various cognitive and neurodegenerative disorders.51 KCNIP3 encoded by calsenilin is a known AD risk factor,52 which was found to be significant in Oli for all two AD-related phenotypes only in cortical layer 5, with FDR = 2.24E−2 for tangle density and FDR = 4.06E−6 for cognitive decline. Additionally, ALCAM, which was differentially expressed in Oli in cortical layer 5 for β-amyloid (FDR = 3.05E−2) and in cortical layer 6 for cognitive decline (FDR = 3.10E−2), has been linked to AD, with elevated plasma levels of ALCAM and vascular cell adhesion protein-1 serving as predictors of AD progression.53

In addition, we utilized the multivariate LMM (mvLMM) as implemented in the GEMMA tool to perform DGE analyses with all three AD-related phenotypes in the same mvLMM.54 For each gene, the Wald test tested whether its expression is differential with respect to at least one of the three phenotypes. We identified a total of 27 LCS unique significant genes with FDR-adjusted q < 0.05 in at least one layer cell-type combination. The FDR is calculated for test p values of all 6 cortical layers per cell type. Among these, 24 genes overlapped with those identified in our single-phenotype LMM-based analyses (Figure S6). Notably, two genes, DPYD and PTPRG, previously significant across all three phenotypes in the single-phenotype analyses, were also replicated by mvLMM in the same cell types. Additionally, of the 10 genes identified as significant in at least 2 phenotypes in the single-phenotype analysis, 7 were replicated by mvLMM within the same cell types (Table S3).

Genes identified by LCS DGE for single AD-related phenotype

Beyond the LCS significant genes identified across multiple phenotypes, several AD-related genes were uniquely associated with single phenotypes. For β-amyloid, we identified a total of 103 significant genes after FDR adjustment, with 59.2% in Mic (Table 3). Remarkably, 86.4% of these significant genes, including several AD-related genes, would have been missed without incorporating spatial information. For instance, the well-established AD-related gene APOE55 was differentially expressed in Mic within cortical layers 5 and 6 for β-amyloid (FDR = 1.98E−2). Similarly, CTSD, which is involved in amyloid precursor protein processing and is considered an AD candidate gene,56 showed significant differential expression only in Mic at cortical layer 6 (FDR = 1.31E−2). Neither APOE nor CTSD were identified by CTS DGE analyses alone.

Discussion

In this study, we designed a robust workflow that integrates spatial ST data with snRNA-seq data to conduct spatially informed CTS DGE analyses by LMM.10 We integrated the cortical layer information in the LIBD ST reference data8 with ROS/MAP snRNA-seq data with a large sample size (n = 436) of DLPFC3,4,5,6 to conduct LCS DGE analyses for three AD-related phenotypes: β-amyloid, tangle density, and cognitive decline. As a result, we identified 138 LCS significant genes, including 103 for β-amyloid, 24 for tangle density, and 25 for cognitive decline. Importantly, traditional CTS DGE analyses without considering cortical layer information failed to detect most of these differentially expressed genes, including 89 for β-amyloid, 14 for tangle density, and 14 for cognitive decline. Notably, APOE, a well-known AD-related gene, was only found to be significant in Mic in cortical layers 5 and 6 for β-amyloid but not by CTS DGE analyses.

By GSEA analyses with the top 500 significant LCS genes for the top 2 cortical layers of the top 2 cell types that have the most significant LCS genes for each phenotype, we found 12 AD-related significant pathways for β-amyloid and 2 for cognitive decline. These 12 AD-related pathways for β-amyloid were detected by using the top significant LCS genes identified in Mic in cortical layer 6, illustrating potential molecular mechanisms through which microglial cells may influence β-amyloid. These findings are crucial for they illuminate specific cellular interactions and molecular pathways that could be targeted for therapeutic interventions for AD. Importantly, all 12 of these AD-related pathways were missed by GSEAs using the CTS DGE analyses results for β-amyloid.

By comparing the LCS significant genes across those 3 AD-related phenotypes, we identified 2 significant genes shared across all three AD-related phenotypes, 5 shared between β-amyloid and cognitive decline, 1 shared between tangle density and cognitive decline, and 4 shared between β-amyloid and tangle density. Several AD-related LCS significant genes that have differential expression in multiple AD-related phenotypes were missed by CTS DGE analysis alone. For example, gene KCNIP3,52 a recognized AD risk factor, was detected as significant in Oli in cortical layer 5 for tangle density and cognitive decline. The most well-known AD-related gene, APOE,55 was only found significant in Mic in cortical layers 5 and 6 for β-amyloid. Our findings show that it is important to study how genes spatially interact with AD phenotypes by considering the spatial information (i.e., cortical layers), which would provide deeper insights into the roles of significant genes in disease progression.

In conclusion, by integrating ST and snRNA-seq data to conduct spatially informed LCS DGE analyses of three AD-related phenotypes, we identified important LCS significant genes with differential expression in specific cell types and cortical layers. These spatially informed LCS DGE results enhance our understanding of the genomic etiology of AD by pinning down differential expression in specific cortical layers and cell types. Important AD-related pathways were found to be enriched with our identified LCS significant genes, and several known AD risk genes (e.g., IL15, CREB5, KCNIP3, ALCAM) were detected only by LCS DGE analyses for multiple AD-related phenotypes. This integration approach addressed the current power limitation of conducting spatially informed DGE analysis with ST data of limited sample sizes or with snRNA-seq data of larger sample sizes but lacking spatial information. This approach not only promises broader applications in both clinical and research settings but it also paves the way for developing more personalized and effective therapeutic strategies for AD.
